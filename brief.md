## Question

Humans detect agency (in a baby, a tiger) the way the eye detects light — a competence already implemented in us, prefiguring an instrument. What is that agency-detector computing, can it be extracted and calibrated into an "agentoscope," and can it be enlarged to find agency where unaided perception cannot (a liver, an organoid), while not being fooled where perception is (weather)?

## Claim

Agency is a model comparison: a system is agentic to the degree its behavior is better predicted by a goal-model than a passive dynamical model, scored A(S) = log P(data|agent)/P(data|passive). The core, counterintuitive result: this score is unreadable from observation — under a constant goal the agent model is a submodel of the passive model, so A <= 0 (AUC = chance). Agency is an interventional property (Pearl's ladder): only perturbation that dissociates goal from mechanism (move the goal, block the path) reveals it. Dynamical richness is a false friend (uncorrelated, even anti-correlated, with agency — the hurricane false positive), and agency is graded, with equifinality-under-obstruction the top criterion. The instrument certifies no consciousness/personhood, is relative to the models compared, needs a boundary, and is silent where perturbation is impossible.
